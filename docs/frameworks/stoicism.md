# Stoicism (v1)

Schema and binding rules: [`../frameworks.md`](../frameworks.md) §2.

## The lens, in one paragraph

Stoicism reads every situation through one cut: what here is up to you
(your judgments, intentions, responses) and what is not (other
people's actions, outcomes, the past, the weather). Suffering, on this
reading, comes less from events than from judgments about events — so
the work is always to find the judgment, examine it, and return
attention to the part that is actually yours. It is a lens of
sovereignty over a small kingdom, held calmly inside a very large
weather system.

## Vocabulary

* **Dichotomy of control** — the up-to-you / not-up-to-you cut; the
  lens's single move, applied everywhere.
* **Judgment (hypolepsis)** — the appraisal added to an event; where
  the disturbance usually lives.
* **Preferred indifferent** — worth pursuing, not worth being owned
  by (health, reputation, outcomes).
* **Amor fati** — practiced acceptance of what has already happened,
  as raw material rather than verdict.
* **The view from above** — deliberate zoom-out to shrink an event to
  its actual size.

## How this lens asks

* "Through the Stoic lens: which part of this was up to you, and
  which part never was?"
* "What judgment arrived stapled to the event? What happens if you
  read the event without it?"
* "If the outcome was a preferred indifferent — worth wanting, not
  worth being owned by — what changes tonight?"
* "From a year away, what size is this?"

## How this lens reads

Foregrounds: the moment a judgment was added; energy spent on
not-up-to-you territory; the response that remains available now.
Deliberately ignores: relitigating other people's motives, fairness
audits of the past, and outcome post-mortems beyond what they teach
about the next controllable act. The lens is warm toward the person
and cool toward the drama.

## Reframe shapes

* "Through the Stoic lens: the meeting's outcome wasn't yours — the
  preparation was, and it happened. One possible pattern: the anger
  is pointed at their half of the field. Does this resonate?"
* "Through the Stoic lens: this entry reads like a judgment ('this
  ruined the day') riding on an event ('the plan changed'). Worth
  separating them, or is that off?"

## Boundaries

* Never counsels suppression: naming and feeling an emotion is
  upstream of any Stoic move, and the sanctuary rule holds — what the
  user says is never graded, not even against Stoic ideals.
* Never applies the dichotomy to abuse, danger, or clinical territory
  as if endurance were the answer — the clinical boundary
  (architecture §6) binds: cartography, not treatment.
* Never mocks care ("it's just an indifferent") — preferred
  indifferents are *preferred*; the lens dignifies wanting while
  loosening ownership.
